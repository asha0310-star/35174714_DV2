Forests for Palm Oil

Malaysia produced 20.28 million tonnes of crude palm oil in 2025. Since 2001, it has also lost about 9.5 million hectares of tree cover. This story shows where the economic gain and forest pressure overlap.

Designed for a general Malaysian audience, this visualisation explains how palm oil growth connects to land expansion, tree-cover loss, and export demand.

Malaysia grew fast, but Indonesia grew faster

Palm oil became the world's dominant vegetable oil in just six decades. Malaysia helped drive that boom, but the crop also pushed plantation land deeper into forested regions. This story follows the trade-off: more oil, more land, and tree-cover loss concentrated in Sabah and Sarawak.

The first chart shows how Malaysia and Indonesia separated from the rest of the world. The bar chart ranks major crude palm oil producers by year, while the line chart shows Malaysia's shrinking share in the longer Our World in Data series. Malaysia's share peaked at about 57% in 1982 and had fallen to about 23% by 2023, even as its own output kept rising.

Malaysia and Indonesia dominate global production, while “Other” correctly aggregates all remaining producers into one line. Data source: Our World in Data — Palm Oil.

Move the producer-ranking slider; the marker on Malaysia's share line follows the same year.

Malaysia remains the world's second-largest palm oil producer, but its global share is falling because Indonesia and other producers expanded faster. This means Malaysia can produce more oil while still becoming less dominant globally. The pressure then shifts from being “how much can Malaysia grow?” to “how can Malaysia grow without losing more forest?”

Malaysia now gets more output from land that has stopped expanding

In 1961, Malaysia had fewer than 55,000 hectares of oil palm. By 2025, MPOB reported 5.70 million hectares planted with oil palm. Most of the expansion came after the 1980s and sped up again after 2000. The key question is where that land came from.

Indonesia’s harvested oil-palm area now exceeds Malaysia’s, and this chart now shows only actual countries rather than income-group aggregates. Data source: Our World in Data.

Tree-cover loss per million tonnes of CPO has fallen since the mid-2010s, suggesting partial decoupling but not proof that palm oil no longer creates forest pressure. Data sources: MPOB 2024 Overview and Global Forest Watch.

More output from existing land

The next step is productivity. Malaysia's crude palm-oil production has continued increasing, while planted-area growth has slowed. That makes yields, replanting, and better use of existing estates more important than simply expanding the plantation footprint.

By indexing both series to 2001 = 100, the chart shows whether output is rising faster than land area without using a dual axis. The 2025 divergence highlights why productivity is central to future growth. Data source: MPOB 2025 Overview, cleaned into the production_area_index.csv file in this repository.

Peninsular Malaysia produces a larger share of CPO than its planted-area share, showing higher output per hectare than East Malaysia. Data source: MPOB 2025 Overview.

Palm expansion and forest loss overlap, then start to diverge

Malaysia's oil-palm footprint expanded steadily, but annual tree-cover loss did not rise in a simple one-for-one pattern. The connected scatter plot below becomes the turning point of the story: it connects land expansion, forest pressure, and time in one view.

How to read this centrepiece:

  • X-axis: oil palm planted area.
  • Y-axis: annual tree-cover loss.
  • Connected points: each point is a year, linked chronologically.
  • Follow the path to see how the relationship evolved through time.

Key insight

The path bends rather than forming a straight upward line. Larger planted area and high tree-cover loss overlap in the early 2010s, but later years move lower on the loss axis. That suggests partial decoupling after the peak years, while still showing that land expansion and forest pressure remain connected.

The connected path shows that larger planted area and high loss often overlap, but post-2014 years sit lower on the loss axis; this is a relationship, not proof that every lost hectare became oil palm. Data sources: MPOB planted-area series and Global Forest Watch tree-cover loss.

States reveal the trade-off more clearly than the national average

A national line can hide geography. The quadrant chart compares each state using its 2025 oil-palm planted area and total tree-cover loss since 2001. The average lines split the chart into four simple zones so readers can see which places carry both economic production and forest pressure.

Takeaway

Sabah and Sarawak sit above both averages, meaning they combine large oil-palm footprints with the largest tree-cover losses. Pahang is the main Peninsular state in the high-loss area, while many smaller states sit in the low-palm, low-loss quadrant.

The state quadrant uses a new small derived file generated by wrangle.py from MPOB 2025 state planted-area figures and existing GFW state totals. Federal territories without MPOB oil-palm planted-area rows are not included in this state palm comparison.

Forest pressure is concentrated in East Malaysia

Between 2001 and 2024, Malaysia lost roughly 9.5 million hectares of tree cover. The geographic story has three parts: first, where total forest loss is highest; second, where loss is most intense relative to the forest area each state started with; and third, where forest loss overlaps with palm-oil production. Tree-cover loss includes commercial harvest and plantation rotations, not only clearing of primary forest.

Absolute loss and relative loss are different measures. Absolute loss counts hectares lost, so large forest states such as Sarawak and Sabah dominate the totals. Relative loss compares loss with each state's 2000 tree-cover extent, so smaller states can rank high even when their hectare totals are much lower.

A. Where is total forest loss highest?

Sarawak and Sabah carry the largest absolute tree-cover losses, making East Malaysia the forest-pressure centre of the story. Map method: the maps use a Mercator projection so regional Malaysia remains familiar and location comparisons are easy to read; the chart focuses on relative state pattern, not precise area measurement. Data source: Global Forest Watch, 2001–2024; darker orange means higher cumulative loss in hectares, not a per-hectare rate.

B. Where is forest loss most intense relative to forest area?

Relative intensity changes the ranking: smaller Peninsular states can appear high because the denominator is their own 2000 tree-cover extent. Sabah and Sarawak remain important hotspots because their relative rates sit near the national average while their absolute hectare losses are very large. Data source: Global Forest Watch, 2001–2024.

Hotspot annotations: Sarawak is the largest absolute loss hotspot, Sabah is both a major forest-loss and palm-oil production hotspot, and together they make East Malaysia the main concentration of forest pressure in this story.

C. What spatial pattern emerges?

Orange forest-loss shading and green CPO-production circles overlap most clearly in Sabah and Sarawak, separating environmental pressure from economic output while keeping both visible. Data sources: Global Forest Watch (forest loss 2001–2024); MPOB 2025 Overview (regional CPO production).

Some states changed rank, but East Malaysia stayed dominant

The heatmap shows the whole time pattern, but rankings make a different question easier: which states moved up or down between the start and the latest year? The slopegraph compares 2001 with 2024 and labels every state directly to reduce legend reading.

Rank 1 means the highest annual tree-cover loss in that year. Kelantan moved up four ranks by 2024, while Negeri Sembilan moved down four; Selangor and Kedah also changed noticeably. Sarawak and Sabah remained the top two loss states. Data source: Global Forest Watch annual tree-cover loss by state.

Annual loss hotspots were strongest in the 2010s

Export demand keeps the pressure on existing estates

In 2025, Malaysia exported 15.27 million tonnes of palm oil worth RM71.63 billion. Palm oil remains a cornerstone of the national economy, even though export volume fell from 2024. Demand is concentrated: seven markets bought 51.5% of Malaysia's palm oil exports. That concentration adds pressure to keep production reliable while meeting stricter sustainability expectations.

Seven import markets account for just over half of Malaysia's palm oil exports, with India clearly the largest single buyer. Data source: MPOB 2025 Overview.

India, Kenya, the European Union, China, Türkiye, the Philippines, and Japan buy 7.86 million tonnes combined, or 51.5% of Malaysia's palm oil exports. Data source: MPOB 2025 Overview, using the cleaned top_importers_2025.csv file in this repository.

The next gains need to come from yield, not new clearing

Malaysia's palm oil boom has created jobs, export revenue, and rural development. It has also contributed to severe forest pressure in the regions where expansion was concentrated. The country has started to respond through replanting, certification, and stronger land-use controls. Tree-cover loss has declined since 2014, but the remaining forest is still valuable and vulnerable.

The practical test is not whether Malaysia can produce palm oil, but whether future growth comes from replanting existing estates rather than converting remaining forest.

Domain, who, why, what, how: This visualisation is about Malaysia's palm oil economy and forest trade-off. It is written for a general Malaysian audience. The purpose is to explain where production, land, tree cover loss, and export demand overlap. The charts combine national time series, regional production data, state-level tree-cover loss, maps, and custom flow/dual-axis idioms using Vega-Lite.
